# Video 42 — Bad Debt — Estimating What You Won't Collect (Allowance Method)
**Chunk:** 42 · **Module:** Module 04 · **Duration target:** 3 min · **Type:** Talking head / instructor
**Learning objective:** GAAP doesn't let you wait until a customer files bankruptcy to record the loss. You estimate it every month.

## Narration (transcript fallback exists in the in-course `video` chunk)

**0:00 —** GAAP doesn't let you wait until a customer files bankruptcy to record the loss. You estimate it every month, in the period you actually earned the revenue — that's the matching principle.

**0:15 —** The allowance method uses two entries. First, *establish the allowance*: estimate a percentage of AR you won't collect and post Dr Bad Debt Expense / Cr Allowance for Doubtful Accounts — a contra-asset that reduces net AR. Skarion estimates 1.5% of $997,700 = $14,965.50.

**0:55 —** Second, *write off* a specific invoice when it's confirmed uncollectible: Dr Allowance for Doubtful Accounts / Cr Accounts Receivable. Notice: **Bad Debt Expense is not touched at write-off** — the expense was already recorded when the allowance was set up.

**1:35 —** The surprise recovery. TeleCom Builders unexpectedly pays $40,000 six months after being written off. You first reverse the write-off (Dr AR $40,000 / Cr Allowance $40,000), then record the cash receipt (Dr Cash $40,000 / Cr AR $40,000). The net effect is Dr Cash $40,000 / Cr Allowance $40,000 — recovery flows back through the allowance, not through revenue.

**2:15 —** Compare this with the *direct write-off* method, which only records bad debt when a specific invoice fails. It's not GAAP-compliant because it books the expense in the wrong period — but small cash-basis firms sometimes use it. Know the difference; an interview will ask.
