# Video 32 — Accrual vs. Cash — The Most Important Concept in Accounting
**Chunk:** 32 · **Module:** Module 02 · **Duration target:** 4 min · **Type:** Talking head / instructor
**Learning objective:** If you only understand one thing from this course, make it this: accounting records economic events when they happen — not when cash moves.

## Narration (transcript fallback exists in the in-course `video` chunk)

**0:00 —** If you only understand one thing from this course, make it this: accounting records economic events when they happen — not when cash moves.

**0:20 —** Here is a real example. Skarion completes a 500-mile HLD package and delivers it to Atlantic Fiber Co. on January 28th. Atlantic Fiber doesn't pay until March 15th. Under cash accounting, we record $480,000 of revenue in March. Under accrual accounting — which is what GAAP requires — we record $480,000 in January, the day we delivered the work.

**1:10 —** Why does this matter? Because cash accounting lies. You could have a wildly profitable January in reality — you delivered millions of dollars of work — but your cash basis P&amp;L shows almost nothing because no one paid you yet. Investors, banks, and your own CFO can't make good decisions from that.

**2:00 —** The flip side is also true. If you prepay $36,000 for your annual Vetro license on July 1st, that is not a $36,000 expense in July. That benefits your entire year. So accrual accounting says: record $3,000 per month as expense, for twelve months. The rest sits on your balance sheet as a Prepaid Asset until you've consumed it.

**3:00 —** This is called the matching principle — match your expenses to the revenue they helped generate. And it is the foundation of every adjusting entry you will ever make during month-end close.
